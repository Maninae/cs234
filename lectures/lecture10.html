Recap: Multi-Armed Bandits and UCB

In the previous lecture, we formalized the multi-armed bandit problem and introduced the Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) algorithm as a principled approach to the exploration-exploitation tradeoff. Recall that a multi-armed bandit is defined by a tuple $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{R})$, where $\mathcal{A}$ is a known set of $K$ actions (arms) and $R_a(r) = P(r \mid a)$ is an unknown reward distribution for each arm. At each time step $t$, the agent selects an action $a_t \in \mathcal{A}$, receives a stochastic reward $r_t \sim R_{a_t}$, and aims to maximize its cumulative reward over $T$ rounds.

The quality of a bandit algorithm is measured by its total regret—the difference between the reward of always playing the best arm and the reward actually collected:

$$\text{Regret}(T) = \sum_{\tau=1}^{T} \mathbb{E}\!\left[\mu^* - r_\tau\right] = T\mu^* - \sum_{\tau=1}^{T} \mathbb{E}\!\left[r_\tau\right]$$

where $\mu^* = \max_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \mu_a$ is the expected reward of the optimal arm. The UCB1 algorithm addresses this by selecting at each step the arm that maximizes an optimistic estimate of its value:

$$a_t = \arg\max_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \left[\hat{Q}_t(a) + \sqrt{\frac{2 \ln t}{N_t(a)}}\right]$$

where $\hat{Q}_t(a)$ is the empirical mean reward and $N_t(a)$ is the number of times arm $a$ has been pulled. The bonus term $\sqrt{2 \ln t / N_t(a)}$ acts as a confidence interval: it is large for rarely-tried arms (encouraging exploration) and shrinks as the arm is pulled more (favoring exploitation of well-understood arms). In this lecture, we rigorously prove that UCB achieves sublinear regret, establish fundamental lower bounds on what any algorithm can achieve, and then extend these ideas from bandits to full MDPs.

Definition — Suboptimality Gap

For each arm $a$, the suboptimality gap (or "gap") is defined as:

$$\Delta_a = \mu^* - \mu_a$$

This is the expected per-step cost of playing arm $a$ instead of the optimal arm. For the optimal arm $a^*$, we have $\Delta_{a^*} = 0$. The gaps $\{\Delta_a\}$ are unknown to the learner but play a central role in regret analysis: they determine how costly each suboptimal pull is and how hard it is to distinguish each arm from the best one.

Decomposing Regret by Arm

The key insight that makes regret analysis tractable is that total regret can be decomposed as a sum over suboptimal arms, each weighted by how often the algorithm plays it. Since pulling the optimal arm incurs zero regret, only suboptimal arms contribute:

$$\text{Regret}(T) = \sum_{a:\, \Delta_a > 0} \Delta_a \cdot \mathbb{E}\!\left[N_a(T)\right]$$

where $N_a(T) = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \mathbf{1}(a_t = a)$ counts how many times arm $a$ is pulled in $T$ rounds. This decomposition is exact and reveals the two factors that drive regret: the gap $\Delta_a$ (how bad the arm is) and the expected pull count $\mathbb{E}[N_a(T)]$ (how often the algorithm is fooled into playing it). A good algorithm must ensure that arms with large gaps are pulled infrequently, and arms with small gaps—which are hard to distinguish from the optimum—contribute little per-pull regret even if they are pulled often.

UCB Regret Bound

Theorem — UCB Regret Bound (Theorem 7.1, Lattimore & Szepesvari)

Consider the UCB algorithm applied to a stochastic $K$-armed bandit with rewards in $[0,1]$. For any horizon $n$, with confidence parameter $\delta = 1/n^2$, the total regret satisfies:

$$\text{Regret}(n) \leq \sum_{i:\, \Delta_i > 0} \left(\frac{16 \ln n}{\Delta_i} + 3\Delta_i\right)$$

In particular, this yields the gap-dependent bound $O\!\left(\sum_{i:\, \Delta_i > 0} \frac{\ln n}{\Delta_i}\right)$ and, by a worst-case argument over the gaps, the gap-independent bound $O\!\left(\sqrt{Kn \ln n}\right)$.

Note — Comparison with Lecture 9

In Lecture 9, we stated the UCB regret bound with a leading constant of 4 rather than 16. Both bounds are correct—the difference arises from the proof technique. The Lecture 9 bound uses a simpler analysis that directly requires the suboptimal arm's UCB to drop below $\mu^*$ after $U_i = 2\ln(1/\delta)/\Delta_i^2$ pulls, yielding a tighter constant. The bound presented here follows Lattimore & Szepesvari's Theorem 7.1, which uses a two-condition good event with a slack parameter $c = 1/2$, requiring the confidence width to shrink to only half the gap. This is more forgiving but increases the required sample count to $u_i = 16\ln n / \Delta_i^2$, producing the larger constant. Both approaches yield $O(\sum_i \ln n / \Delta_i)$ regret—only the leading constant differs.

This theorem tells us two important things. First, the regret grows only logarithmically in the time horizon $n$—dramatically better than the linear regret suffered by naive strategies like pure exploration or pure exploitation. Second, the bound depends on the inverse gaps $1/\Delta_i$: arms that are very close to optimal are harder to rule out and require more samples, contributing more to the regret. We now sketch the proof.

Proof Sketch: Bounding $\mathbb{E}[N_i(n)]$ for Suboptimal Arms

The proof proceeds by bounding $\mathbb{E}[N_i(n)]$—the expected number of times each suboptimal arm $i$ is pulled—and then plugging these bounds back into the regret decomposition. Without loss of generality, assume arm 1 is optimal, so $a^* = a_1$.

Proof Sketch

Step 1: Define the "good event." For each suboptimal arm $i$, define the good event $G_i$ as the intersection of two conditions:

Condition (*): The UCB for the optimal arm always exceeds its true mean:

$$\mu_1 \leq \min_{t \leq n}\, \text{UCB}_1(t, \delta) \quad \text{where } \text{UCB}_a(t, \delta) = \hat{Q}_t(a) + \sqrt{\frac{2\ln(1/\delta)}{N_t(a)}}$$

Condition (**): For a carefully chosen number of pulls $u_i$, the UCB of arm $i$ falls below the true mean of arm 1:

$$\hat{\mu}_{i,u_i} + \sqrt{\frac{2\ln(1/\delta)}{u_i}} < \mu_1$$

Intuitively, when both conditions hold, the algorithm can distinguish arm $i$ from the optimum after $u_i$ pulls: the optimistic estimate for arm $i$ drops below what we know arm 1 can achieve.

Step 2: Bound pulls under the good event. We claim that when $G_i$ holds, arm $i$ cannot be pulled more than $u_i$ times. The argument is by contradiction: suppose arm $i$ has already been pulled $u_i$ times and is selected again at time $t$. Then by condition (**), $\text{UCB}_i(t, \delta) < \mu_1$, and by condition (*), $\mu_1 \leq \text{UCB}_1(t, \delta)$. Chaining these inequalities gives $\text{UCB}_i(t, \delta) < \text{UCB}_1(t, \delta)$, which means the algorithm should have selected arm 1 instead of arm $i$—a contradiction. Therefore:

$$\mathbb{E}\!\left[\mathbf{1}(G_i)\, N_i(n)\right] \leq u_i$$

Step 3: Bound the probability of the bad event. We decompose the expected pull count using the law of total expectation:

$$\mathbb{E}[N_i(n)] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}(G_i)\, N_i(n)] + \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}(G_i^c)\, N_i(n)] \leq u_i + n \cdot P(G_i^c)$$

To bound $P(G_i^c)$, we analyze each condition separately. For condition (*), by the union bound over all time steps and applying the concentration inequality (Hoeffding's inequality), we get:

$$P\!\left(\mu_1 > \min_{t \leq n} \text{UCB}_1(t, \delta)\right) \leq \sum_{s=1}^{n} P\!\left(\mu_1 > \hat{\mu}_{1,s} + \sqrt{\frac{2\ln(1/\delta)}{s}}\right) \leq n\delta$$

For condition (**), we use Hoeffding's inequality applied to the empirical mean after $u_i$ pulls. Provided $u_i$ is large enough that $\sqrt{2\ln(1/\delta)/u_i} \leq c\Delta_i$ for some $c \in (0,1)$, the probability of condition (**) failing is bounded by:

$$P\!\left(\hat{\mu}_{i,u_i} + \sqrt{\frac{2\ln(1/\delta)}{u_i}} \geq \mu_1\right) \leq \exp\!\left(-\frac{u_i\, c^2 \Delta_i^2}{2}\right)$$

Step 4: Choose $u_i$ and combine. The constraint $\sqrt{2\ln(1/\delta)/u_i} \leq c\Delta_i$ rearranges to:

$$u_i \geq \frac{2\ln(1/\delta)}{c^2 \Delta_i^2}$$

Setting $c = 1/2$ and $\delta = 1/n^2$, so that $\ln(1/\delta) = 2\ln n$, yields:

$$u_i = \frac{16\ln n}{\Delta_i^2}$$

Substituting back into the bound on $\mathbb{E}[N_i(n)]$ and combining the failure probabilities gives:

$$\mathbb{E}[N_i(n)] \leq \frac{16\ln n}{\Delta_i^2} + 3$$

Plugging into the regret decomposition $\text{Regret}(n) = \sum_{i:\Delta_i > 0} \Delta_i \cdot \mathbb{E}[N_i(n)]$ yields:

$$\text{Regret}(n) \leq \sum_{i:\,\Delta_i > 0} \left(\frac{16\ln n}{\Delta_i} + 3\Delta_i\right)$$

which completes the proof. $\square$

Insight — Why Logarithmic Regret is Achievable

The proof reveals the mechanism behind logarithmic regret. Each suboptimal arm $i$ needs roughly $O(\ln n / \Delta_i^2)$ pulls before its confidence interval is tight enough to conclusively rule it out. After that point, the algorithm almost never selects it again. The total regret contribution from arm $i$ is therefore $\Delta_i \times O(\ln n / \Delta_i^2) = O(\ln n / \Delta_i)$. The logarithmic dependence on $n$ arises because the confidence bonus $\sqrt{\ln t / N_t(a)}$ shrinks at a rate that exactly balances the growing number of rounds. Essentially, the algorithm needs to "waste" only a logarithmically growing number of pulls on each suboptimal arm to be confident it is suboptimal.

The Gap-Independent Bound

The gap-dependent bound $O(\sum_i \ln n / \Delta_i)$ is tight when the gaps are known, but it can be vacuous when some gaps are very small. We can derive a gap-independent (also called "minimax") bound by a worst-case argument. For each arm $i$, the contribution $\Delta_i \cdot \mathbb{E}[N_i(n)]$ is bounded by $16 \ln n / \Delta_i + 3\Delta_i$. To find the worst case, we note that the sum is maximized when the gaps are all equal: $\Delta_i = \Delta$ for all $K-1$ suboptimal arms. Then:

$$\text{Regret}(n) \leq (K-1)\left(\frac{16\ln n}{\Delta} + 3\Delta\right)$$

Optimizing over $\Delta$ by setting the derivative to zero yields $\Delta^* = \sqrt{16\ln n / 3}$, but we must also respect $\Delta \leq 1$. Substituting back gives a bound of order $O(\sqrt{Kn\ln n})$, which depends on neither the gaps nor the specific reward distributions.

Lower Bounds on Bandit Regret

Theorem — Bandit Regret Lower Bound (Lai & Robbins, 1985)

For any consistent bandit algorithm (one whose regret is $o(n^p)$ for all $p > 0$ on every bandit instance), the expected regret on a $K$-armed bandit satisfies:

$$\liminf_{n \to \infty} \frac{\text{Regret}(n)}{\ln n} \geq \sum_{i:\, \Delta_i > 0} \frac{\Delta_i}{D_{\text{KL}}(\nu_i \,\|\, \nu^*)}$$

where $D_{\text{KL}}(\nu_i \| \nu^*)$ is the KL divergence between the reward distribution of arm $i$ and the optimal arm. For Gaussian rewards with unit variance, this simplifies to:

$$\liminf_{n \to \infty} \frac{\text{Regret}(n)}{\ln n} \geq \sum_{i:\, \Delta_i > 0} \frac{2}{\Delta_i}$$

The lower bound shows that UCB's $O(\ln n / \Delta_i)$ scaling per arm is order-optimal—no algorithm can do fundamentally better. The intuition is information-theoretic: to distinguish arm $i$ from the optimum, the algorithm must collect enough samples to differentiate their reward distributions, which requires $\Omega(1/D_{\text{KL}}(\nu_i \| \nu^*))$ pulls. Each of these pulls incurs $\Delta_i$ regret.

In the gap-independent (minimax) setting, the corresponding lower bound is:

$$\text{Regret}(n) = \Omega\!\left(\sqrt{Kn}\right)$$

Since UCB achieves $O(\sqrt{Kn\ln n})$, the gap to the lower bound is only a $\sqrt{\ln n}$ factor. Algorithms like MOSS (Minimax Optimal Strategy in the Stochastic case) and certain variants of UCB close this gap entirely, achieving the minimax-optimal $O(\sqrt{Kn})$ rate. Nevertheless, UCB remains popular because of its simplicity and strong practical performance.

Insight — The Exploration-Information Tradeoff

The lower bound exposes a deep connection between regret minimization and hypothesis testing. An algorithm must gather enough statistical evidence to distinguish the optimal arm from each suboptimal alternative. The KL divergence quantifies the "statistical distance" between two reward distributions: when distributions are close (small KL divergence), more samples are needed to tell them apart, and more regret is incurred. This information-theoretic perspective unifies exploration across bandits, MDPs, and Bayesian optimization.

From Bandits to MDPs: The Exploration Challenge

Multi-armed bandits are a powerful abstraction, but real-world reinforcement learning problems involve sequential decision-making: actions affect not only the immediate reward but also the next state, which in turn determines future actions and rewards. A $K$-armed bandit is, in essence, a single-state MDP with $K$ actions. Moving to full MDPs introduces several new challenges for exploration:

  • State-dependent exploration: The agent must explore different actions in different states. An action that is well-understood in one state may be completely untested in another.
  • Delayed consequences: Trying a suboptimal action may lead to an informative state that enables future learning. The value of information is spread across time, not concentrated at the moment of decision.
  • Exponential state spaces: With $|\mathcal{S}|$ states and $|\mathcal{A}|$ actions, the number of state-action pairs grows multiplicatively, making exhaustive exploration costly.
  • Non-stationary exploration needs: As the agent learns more about some parts of the state space, it should shift exploration to less-understood regions—but reaching those regions may require traversing well-understood territory.

These challenges motivate formal frameworks for measuring and guaranteeing exploration efficiency in MDPs. The two dominant frameworks are regret minimization (extending the bandit notion to MDPs) and PAC-MDP (bounding the number of suboptimal actions).

PAC-MDP: Probably Approximately Correct in MDPs

Definition — PAC-MDP

An algorithm $\mathfrak{A}$ is PAC-MDP (Probably Approximately Correct for MDPs) if, for any $\epsilon > 0$ and $\delta > 0$, with probability at least $1 - \delta$, the number of time steps $t$ at which $\mathfrak{A}$ takes an action that is not $\epsilon$-optimal is bounded by a polynomial in $(|\mathcal{S}|, |\mathcal{A}|, 1/\epsilon, 1/\delta, 1/(1-\gamma))$. Formally, the sample complexity of exploration $N_\epsilon$ satisfies:

$$P\!\left(\left|\{t : V^{\pi_t}(s_t) < V^*(s_t) - \epsilon \}\right| > N_\epsilon \right) \leq \delta$$

where $\pi_t$ is the policy executed by the algorithm at time $t$.

The PAC-MDP framework differs from regret minimization in an important way. Regret measures the total cost of suboptimal actions, weighting each action by its degree of suboptimality. PAC-MDP instead counts the number of $\epsilon$-suboptimal actions, treating all such actions equally. A PAC-MDP algorithm guarantees that, after a polynomial "burn-in" period of exploration, it acts near-optimally for all remaining time steps. This is a strong guarantee: it says the algorithm eventually "figures things out" and stops making costly mistakes, with high probability.

Insight — PAC-MDP vs. Regret

A PAC-MDP bound of $N_\epsilon$ suboptimal steps implies a regret bound of roughly $O(N_\epsilon)$ (each suboptimal step contributes at most $O(V_{\max})$ regret). However, the conversion is often loose: PAC-MDP bounds tend to be polynomially larger than the best regret bounds. Conversely, low-regret algorithms are not always PAC-MDP, because regret bounds allow many mildly-suboptimal actions as long as their total cost is controlled. The two frameworks offer complementary perspectives on exploration quality.

Model-Based Exploration: Optimism in MDPs

The principle of "optimism in the face of uncertainty" that powered UCB in bandits extends naturally to MDPs. The idea is simple: when the agent does not know the true MDP dynamics, it should assume the dynamics are as favorable as possible (consistent with the data), and act optimally with respect to this optimistic model. This encourages the agent to visit unfamiliar states, because those states might harbor high rewards under the optimistic assumption.

R-MAX

R-MAX (Brafman and Tennenholtz, 2002) is one of the earliest provably efficient exploration algorithms for MDPs. It classifies each state-action pair as either known (visited sufficiently often) or unknown, and constructs an optimistic model:

Algorithm: R-MAX
  1. Initialize: Mark all state-action pairs $(s, a)$ as unknown. Set visit threshold $m$.
  2. Construct optimistic MDP $\tilde{M}$:
    1. For known $(s,a)$ (visited $\geq m$ times): use empirical transition $\hat{P}(s' \mid s, a)$ and empirical reward $\hat{R}(s, a)$.
    2. For unknown $(s,a)$: set reward to $R_{\max}$ (maximum possible reward) and transitions to a self-absorbing state with reward $R_{\max}$.
  3. Plan: Compute the optimal policy $\tilde{\pi}^*$ for the optimistic MDP $\tilde{M}$ (via value iteration).
  4. Act: Execute $\tilde{\pi}^*$ in the real environment. Update visit counts and empirical estimates.
  5. Repeat: When any $(s,a)$ transitions from unknown to known, re-plan on the updated $\tilde{M}$.

The key mechanism is that unknown state-action pairs are maximally attractive in the optimistic MDP. The optimal policy for $\tilde{M}$ will therefore seek out unknown pairs—driving exploration—until they become known. Once a pair is known, its reward and dynamics in $\tilde{M}$ match reality (approximately), so the policy for $\tilde{M}$ is near-optimal in the real MDP for the known portion of the state space.

Theorem — R-MAX Sample Complexity

R-MAX is PAC-MDP. With an appropriate choice of the threshold $m$, the number of $\epsilon$-suboptimal time steps is at most:

$$N_\epsilon = O\!\left(\frac{|\mathcal{S}|^2 |\mathcal{A}|}{\epsilon^3 (1-\gamma)^6} \ln\frac{1}{\delta} \ln\frac{1}{\epsilon(1-\gamma)}\right)$$

This is polynomial in all relevant quantities, guaranteeing efficient exploration.

Explicit Explore or Exploit (E3)

The E3 algorithm (Kearns and Singh, 2002) takes a slightly different approach. Rather than building a single optimistic MDP, E3 maintains a set of "known" states and explicitly decides between two modes: exploitation (following the best known policy within the known region) and exploration (deliberately seeking unknown states). E3 computes a "balanced wandering" policy that is guaranteed to either collect high reward or quickly reach an unknown state, ensuring that exploration makes steady progress. Like R-MAX, E3 achieves polynomial sample complexity, though with somewhat different polynomial dependence on the problem parameters.

Optimistic Initialization

A simpler, heuristic form of optimism is optimistic initialization: initialize the value function $Q(s,a)$ or $V(s)$ to an optimistically high value (e.g., $V_{\max} = R_{\max}/(1-\gamma)$) before any learning begins. Combined with standard model-free algorithms like Q-learning, this creates a natural drive to explore: the agent believes unvisited state-action pairs have very high value and preferentially visits them. As it accumulates experience, the value estimates decrease toward their true values, and the exploratory pressure fades.

Optimistic initialization is easy to implement and often effective in practice, but it lacks the formal guarantees of R-MAX or E3. In particular, it does not ensure that every state-action pair is visited a sufficient number of times, and in large or adversarial environments, the initial optimism can decay before the agent has explored adequately. Despite these limitations, optimistic initialization remains a widely-used exploration strategy because of its simplicity and compatibility with existing algorithms.

Sample Complexity of Exploration

The sample complexity of exploration provides a unified lens through which to compare exploration algorithms. It asks: how many time steps of suboptimal behavior must an algorithm endure before it converges to near-optimal performance? The answer depends critically on the structure of the MDP.

For tabular MDPs with $|\mathcal{S}|$ states and $|\mathcal{A}|$ actions, the best known PAC-MDP bounds scale as:

$$N_\epsilon = \tilde{O}\!\left(\frac{|\mathcal{S}|^2 |\mathcal{A}|}{\epsilon^2(1-\gamma)^4}\right)$$

where $\tilde{O}$ hides logarithmic factors. This is polynomial in all quantities, confirming that efficient exploration is possible. For regret minimization in episodic MDPs, the best known algorithms (such as UCBVI) achieve:

$$\text{Regret}(T) = \tilde{O}\!\left(\sqrt{|\mathcal{S}||\mathcal{A}| H^3 T}\right)$$

where $H$ is the episode length and $T$ is the total number of episodes. This matches known lower bounds up to logarithmic factors, establishing that the exploration problem in tabular MDPs is essentially solved in theory.

Insight — The Bandit-MDP Connection

The progression from bandits to MDPs reveals a recurring theme: the principle of optimism in the face of uncertainty is remarkably versatile. In bandits, UCB adds a bonus to the estimated reward of each arm. In MDPs, R-MAX adds a bonus to the reward of unknown state-action pairs. In both cases, optimism drives systematic exploration by making the agent's estimates of poorly-understood options favorably biased, ensuring they are tried before being discarded. The formal analysis follows the same template too: decompose regret by identifying where suboptimality occurs, bound the frequency of suboptimal decisions using concentration inequalities and the optimistic structure, and sum over all sources of regret. The core mathematical machinery—Hoeffding bounds, union bounds, and careful counting arguments—transfers directly from bandits to MDPs, with the main complication being the coupling between states across time.

Summary

This lecture established the theoretical foundations of fast reinforcement learning. We began by proving that the UCB algorithm achieves $O\!\left(\sum_{i:\Delta_i > 0} \ln n / \Delta_i\right)$ regret in the multi-armed bandit setting, with the proof hinging on a decomposition of regret by arm, a "good event" analysis based on Hoeffding concentration, and a careful choice of the number of exploration pulls $u_i$ for each suboptimal arm. We then showed that this logarithmic rate is essentially optimal: the Lai-Robbins lower bound demonstrates that no algorithm can achieve $o(\ln n)$ regret in the worst case, and the minimax lower bound is $\Omega(\sqrt{Kn})$.

Turning to MDPs, we introduced the PAC-MDP framework as a formal measure of exploration efficiency: an algorithm is PAC-MDP if it takes at most polynomially many suboptimal actions. We studied R-MAX and E3 as concrete examples of provably efficient model-based exploration algorithms, both building on the principle of optimism in the face of uncertainty. We also discussed optimistic initialization as a simpler but less theoretically grounded alternative. The sample complexity of exploration in tabular MDPs is now well understood, with matching upper and lower bounds establishing that polynomial-time efficient exploration is achievable.

In the next lecture, we will explore Bayesian approaches to bandits and MDPs, including Thompson sampling and Bayesian regret bounds, which offer an alternative to the frequentist optimism-based methods studied here.
